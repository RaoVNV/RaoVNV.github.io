Welcome to my education page! Below, you will find more information about each of the programs of study I have undertaken, and the advisors I have worked with.

PhD in Educational Psychology (Statistics Education)

After being asked to redesign an introductory statistics course (GWU HSCI 2117; see my teaching for more), I sought to make evidence-based decisions and thus began searching the statistics education literature. This search quickly led me to Dr. Joan Garfield, Dr. Robert delMas (Bob), and Dr. Andrew Zieffler (Andy) at the University of Minnesota. After meeting Bob, Andy, and some of their current and former students during a visit to Minneapolis, I decided to study statistics education under their direction.

My early milestone work focused on psychometric analyses of the Comprehensive Assessment of Outcomes in Statistics (CAOS). I then shifted to studying students’ understanding of statistical testing for my dissertation, especially their understanding of sampling distributions under a null hypothesis.

MA in Educational Psychology (Learning and Cognition)

While earning my PhD, I began taking courses from faculty in the Psychological Foundations of Education program in the Department of Educational Psychology. At this time I also began working with Dr. Sashank Varma and Dr. Jeffery Bye on two different projects, and participated in their lab.

To improve my knowledge and skills in the cognitive and learning sciences, I began study under the direction of Dr. David DeLiema and Dr. Jeffery Bye, focusing on courses such as Mathematical Cognition, Computational Thinking, and Advanced Cognition.

MS in Statistics

While working for the US Census Bureau, I pursued an MS in Statistics from the George Washington University part-time. My coursework primarily focused on modern data analysis and applied statistics, feature courses such as Data Mining, Legal Statistics, and Modern Theory of Sample Surveys.

I worked on some small projects with Dr. J.L. Gastwirth and Dr. Rida Moustafa, but due to competing priorities from my job (and given that I was not, at that time, considering a career in academia or even pursuing a PhD), I never presenting this work at conferences or produced manuscripts.
